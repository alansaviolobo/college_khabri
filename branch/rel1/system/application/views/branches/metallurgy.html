Overview

Metallurgical engineers extract, refine, and recycle metals. They solve problems such as reducing corrosion, maintaining heat levels, and increasing the strength of a product. They help develop or improve metals that are used in healthcare, in transportation, in defense, and in the entertainment industry. The work of a metallurgical engineer impacts most other engineering fields as they develop core materials that can enhance the function of many products and systems. Metallurgical engineering students take courses such as Thermodynamics, Physical Metallurgy, Corrosion Engineering, Powder Metallurgy, Hydrometallurgy, and Electrometallurgy.

Employment/Earnings

Metallurgical engineers are employed in many industries and work for companies refine, produce, manufacture and distribute metals. Because metals are used in so many industries, employment can be in large companies or small consulting firms. Salary ranges for metallurgical engineers are usually categorized under the materials engineering discipline in salary surveys. There are very few accredited metallurgical engineering programs, and so employment rates for those graduating with a bachelors degree in metallurgical engineering are are extremely high.

Career Path Forecast

Metallurgical engineers can expect to have employment growth about as fast as the average for all occupations. Metallurgical engineers will be needed to develop new materials for electronics, biotechnology, and other areas.
